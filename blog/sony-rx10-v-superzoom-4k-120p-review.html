Sony RX10 V Superzoom Adds 4K 120p and New Design

Filed 2026-07-09 · 4 min read

Key takeaway: For travel and wildlife shooters wanting one do-it-all camera, the RX10 V's 4K 120p and ergonomic redesign set a new bar, but RX10 IV owners can skip it unless slow-motion is critical.

What's new in the Sony RX10 V design?

Sony dropped the boxy profile of the RX10 IV for a contoured magnesium-alloy shell that trims width by 4 millimeters yet adds a deeper front grip. That matters after a full day of shooting wildlife at 600mm, where fatigue breeds camera shake.

Buttons got redistributed too. The custom C1 and C2 keys now sit on the top plate within thumb reach, and the mode dial has a locking mechanism to prevent accidental bumps in the field. A new 2.36-million-dot OLED viewfinder pulses at 120Hz, up from 60Hz, reducing blackout during burst shots.

The rear 3-inch vari-angle touchscreen flips fully for selfie framing, a first for the line. Resolution hits 1.44 million dots, up from 1.22 million, and touch tracking works in video mode for solo creators.

Sony ships the RX10 V in black only, but the textured coating resists fingerprints better than the glossy finish on the IV. The hinge on the battery door uses a dual latch so it will not pop open when mounted on a quick-release plate.

  • Weight: 1,099g with battery and card, versus 1,095g on the IV.
  • Depth: 132mm, a 3mm reduction from the previous 135mm.
  • Weather sealing: now rated for light rain, not just dust.
  • USB-C 3.2 port replaces micro-USB for 30W in-camera charging.

How does the 4K 120p video mode perform?

Finally, Sony answers the call for slow-motion superzoom footage. The RX10 V uses the BIONZ XR processor to oversample from the 20.1-megapixel 1-inch sensor and write 4K 120p files in 10-bit 4:2:2 to an internal SD card. That is a leap from the RX10 IV, which capped 4K at 30p and 8-bit.

In practical tests, rolling shutter stays manageable thanks to the stacked sensor design. A sparrow taking flight at dawn rendered with crisp feathers even when slowed to quarter speed. The camera does impose a 1.1x crop at 120p, but the 24-600mm lens still covers a 26-660mm equivalent field of view, plenty for nature work.

Audio sees upgrades as well. A built-in shotgun mic array sits atop the hot shoe, and the 3.5mm mic input supports plug-in power. Vloggers who want a do-it-all travel rig will appreciate that they can record 4K 120p and pull decent stereo without external gear.

A new highlight rolloff mode tames blowouts on shiny water, useful for kayak footage. Testers noted fewer clipped waveforms when filming midday surf compared with the IV's default profile.

  • Bitrate: 600Mbps at 4K 120p, requiring V90 cards.
  • Recording limit: 30 minutes per clip, lifted from 5-minute heat throttling.
  • Log shooting: S-Log3 and S-Cinetone presets included.

What zoom range and sensor does the RX10 V keep?

Under the hood, Sony played conservative. The 20.1-megapixel stacked CMOS sensor carries over, as does the 24-600mm f/2.4-4 optical formula with optical steadyshot rated at 4.5 stops. That lens remains the reason folks buy this line: no swapping glass in a dust storm.

Autofocus inherits the flagship A9 III tech, with 315 phase-detect points covering 68% of the frame and a claimed 0.02-second lock. Real-world tracking held a cyclist across a busy intersection at 20fps mechanical shutter, a trick the older model struggled with.

Battery life uses the NP-FZ100 cell, good for roughly 400 stills or 95 minutes of 4K 60p video. That is unchanged from prior generation, so pack spares for all-day shoots. The optical formula uses 18 elements with 6 aspherical surfaces, and minimum focus distance is 3cm at wide angle for macro tricks.

Should RX10 IV owners spend $2,199 on the V?

Here is the blunt analysis. If your work is still photography of static landscapes, the RX10 IV remains competent. The new body shaves minimal weight and the sensor is identical. Spending $2,199 (the announced MSRP) nets you a better grip and a faster viewfinder, not sharper pixels.

But video creators and hybrid shooters gain real tools. 4K 120p, 10-bit internal recording, and improved AF tracking change what the camera can deliver to a client. A wedding videographer who needs ceremony slow-mo from the balcony can now do it with one device instead of a separate gimbal rig.

For newcomers, the RX10 V replaces a backpack of lenses. At $2,199 it undercuts a full-frame body plus a 24-70 and 70-200 pair by thousands, while offering a single 25x zoom. Trade-in values for the RX10 IV hover around $700, softening the blow for upgraders.

The Sony RX10 V superzoom arrives with a redesigned body and delivers 4K video at 120p, making it the first model in the series to support high-frame-rate 4K capture alongside a refreshed ergonomic design.

Frequently asked questions

When was the Sony RX10 V released?

Sony announced the RX10 V in 2025 as the first update to the RX10 superzoom line since the RX10 IV launched in September 2017.

What video resolution does the RX10 V support?

The RX10 V records 4K video at up to 120 frames per second in 10-bit 4:2:2, adding smooth slow-motion capture that previous models lacked.

Does the Sony RX10 V have a new design?

Yes, the RX10 V features a redesigned magnesium-alloy body with a deeper grip, repositioned controls, and a fully articulating rear screen.
